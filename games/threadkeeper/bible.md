# The Thread-Keeper — story bible

PREMISE. Wei's village drowned when the river sluice failed in a spring flood. She
climbs to the Drowned Monastery in the Reed Marsh, where an old order keeps the Loom
of Names — a frame on which every life in the valley hangs as a thread. She wants one
answer: could her village have been saved? What she finds is the power, once, to cut,
mend, or leave a single thread when the flood comes again.

VOICE. Second person, present tense for action, past for memory. Cold water, reed,
wet rope, smoke. Grief held flat, not wailed. One sensory anchor per scene. Wuxia
restraint: power is quiet, costs are stated plainly, nobody monologues their feelings.

CHARACTERS.
- Wei (you): a disciple of nothing, grief-driven, sharp-eyed. Wants blame as much as truth.
- Old Bo: the marsh ferryman. Poles pilgrims across. Was the valley's sluice-keeper the
  year of the flood, and has been ferrying penance ever since. Introduced at the ferry.
- Master Yue: the last keeper of the Loom of Names. Blind, thread-scarred, unsentimental.
  Will not choose for Wei. Introduced in the hall.

PLACES. The Reed Marsh — flooded paddies and drowned bell-towers below the monastery.
The Drowned Monastery — half-sunk, reached only by Bo's flat-bottomed boat.

LORE. The Loom of Names — a great wooden frame strung with threads, one per living
soul in the valley. To cut a thread ends a life; to mend it binds a fate; to leave it
is to let the river decide. The order keeps it and is forbidden to use it. Mostly.

MOTIFS. Water, thread, reed, weight, blame, the line between mercy and meddling.

CHOICE SPACE. Three endings — cut the enemy's thread (vengeance), mend the broken
village-thread (restoration at a price), or leave the loom untouched (acceptance).
